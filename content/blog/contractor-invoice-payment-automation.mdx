**56% of U.S. small businesses are currently owed money on unpaid invoices.** The average amount sitting uncollected: **$17,500 per business**. And nearly half of those invoices are more than 30 days overdue — not in dispute, not forgotten, just unpaid because nobody followed up.

For contractors — HVAC, plumbing, electrical, remodeling — the numbers are worse. **82% of contractors now wait 30 or more days past their expected payment date** to receive funds, up from 49% just two years ago. The businesses closing this gap aren't hiring collection staff or getting harder on customers. They've automated three things: when the invoice goes out, how payment is collected on-site, and what happens when payment doesn't come. ## Why Contractor Payment Collection Breaks Down

The root cause of slow payment isn't customer reluctance. It's delay at every step of the invoicing process — delays that compound each other.

**Invoices go out late.** The job finishes Tuesday. The crew packs up, drives to the next site, and the invoice gets generated on Friday — or the following Monday — when someone has time to sit down and do paperwork. Research from field service analytics platforms shows that invoices sent within 10 days of job completion result in an average payment time of **51 days**. Invoices sent 20 or more days after the job take an average of **85 days**. That 10-day delay costs you 34 days of cash flow. On a job that invoices for $4,000, those 34 days represent real working capital you don't have.

**No online payment option exists.** A large portion of contractors still collect by check or rely on customers to initiate a bank transfer. Both create friction that costs days. Customers who would pay immediately if handed a link don't always remember to write a check. It's not bad faith — it's inconvenience. Every step you add between "invoice received" and "payment made" costs you time.

**There's no systematic reminder process.** Most shops rely on someone to notice an invoice has aged and then manually call or email the customer. That process works until the shop is busy — which is exactly when the cash flow pressure from slow invoices is highest. **1 in 3 businesses spend 30 or more hours per month on manual payment tasks.** That's nearly a full work week every month consumed by invoice tracking that automation handles in the background.

**The culture is cash or check.** In the trades, on-site cash collection was the norm for decades. The shift to digital payment has lagged other industries, which means many contractors haven't built the systems that make collecting by card or ACH as frictionless as handing someone an invoice and asking if they have a check.

The result of these four compounding factors: **64% of specialty trade contractors regularly experience slow payment**, and **56% of subcontractors have turned down work specifically because of cash flow risk** — not because they lacked the capacity or skill, but because they couldn't afford to front materials and labor on another slow-paying job.

## Automate the Invoice: From Job Complete to Payment Link in Minutes

The single highest-leverage change in contractor payment collection is sending the invoice the same day the job closes — and making it clickable.

Here's what that workflow looks like when it's automated:

**Step 1: Tech marks the job complete in the field service platform.** Using Jobber, Housecall Pro, or ServiceTitan, the technician closes the job on their phone from the job site. This is a status change that takes ten seconds.

**Step 2: The invoice generates automatically.** The platform creates the invoice from the job record — pulling in line items, parts, labor hours, and any notes already logged during the visit. No one types anything. No one builds a document. It exists the moment the job is marked complete.

**Step 3: The invoice delivers via SMS and email within minutes.** Both channels, simultaneously. The customer gets a notification on their phone and an email with a direct "Pay Now" link. The link opens a mobile-friendly payment page that accepts credit card, debit card, or ACH transfer. No login required. No portal to navigate. One tap, payment submitted.

**Contractors who invoice within 24 hours of job completion get paid an average of 14 days sooner** than contractors who wait a week or more. At an average invoice of $1,500 to $5,000, that's 14 days of working capital per job that materializes faster — across every job you complete.

For on-site payment — the finish line for jobs where the homeowner or business owner is present at completion — Housecall Pro's Instapay feature allows techs to collect card payment on the spot and receive the funds the same day, not the next business day. That's cash in your account before the truck is back at the shop.

For repeat customers and service agreements, the automation adds a card-on-file layer: the customer authorizes recurring billing once, and every future invoice charges automatically without them doing anything. **Recurring job automation eliminates the invoice collection cycle entirely** for your most loyal customers — the people who are least likely to want the friction of paying manually.

## The Payment Reminder Sequence That Moves Money

Even with same-day invoicing and clean payment links, some invoices age. A homeowner travels for work. A property manager has AP on a cycle. A commercial customer's check gets stuck in their own approvals process. None of these are bad actors — they just need the right nudge at the right time.

A systematic reminder sequence recovers that money without anyone making an uncomfortable phone call. Here's what a high-performing sequence looks like:

**Day 0 — Invoice delivery:** SMS + email with payment link, summarizing the job completed and total due.

**Day 3 — Soft check-in (SMS):** *"Hi [Name], just making sure you received our invoice for the work completed on [date]. Here's the payment link for your convenience: [link]. Let us know if you have any questions."* Friendly, not urgent. Assumes good faith.

**Day 7 (due date or 7 days out) — Due date reminder (email):** Clear notification that payment is due or approaching. Restate the total, include the payment link, and offer a phone number for questions. This email reads as administrative, not pressuring.

**Day 3 past due — First past-due notice (SMS + email):** The tone shifts slightly to practical: *"Your invoice for $[amount] is now 3 days past due. You can pay in under two minutes here: [link]. If there's an issue with the invoice, reply to this message."* Opening the door to questions resolves disputes faster than letting them sit.

**Day 10 past due — Escalation (email + personal call flag):** Email restates the outstanding balance and references prior reminders. In your platform, a task fires for a team member to make a personal call. This is no longer automated — it's a human touch for accounts that need one.

**Day 21 past due — Final notice:** Clear statement that the account is overdue, with a deadline. For commercial accounts, reference your lien rights if applicable (many field service platforms generate lien notices automatically).

Every sequence pauses the moment payment is received. No customer who paid on Day 3 gets a Day 7 reminder. The system reads the payment status in real time and stops.

The data on this approach is consistent: **automated payment reminders reduce average collection time from 45 days to 15–20 days**. Adding SMS to email-only reminder workflows increases the likelihood of being paid within a week of the invoice due date by **56%**. Companies using automated AR sequences report **15% fewer bad debt write-offs** and **22% lower days sales outstanding** versus those relying on manual follow-up.

For a contractor running $80,000 to $200,000 per month in revenue, a 22% reduction in days sales outstanding is meaningful. It's the difference between a business that can take on a large job with upfront material costs and one that has to turn it down.

## What to Track Once It's Running

Five numbers tell you whether your payment automation is working:

1. **Average days to payment** — from invoice sent to payment received. Baseline this before you automate. Target: **under 21 days** for residential work, under 35 days for commercial. Most contractors without automation are sitting at 45–60 days.

2. **Same-day or next-day invoice rate** — what percentage of completed jobs have an invoice sent within 24 hours of job close. Target: **90%+**. Track this weekly for the first month. Anything below 70% means the job completion workflow isn't triggering the invoice correctly.

3. **Overdue invoice percentage** — invoices past their due date as a share of total outstanding AR. Target: **under 15%**. If this climbs above 25%, your reminder sequence isn't firing or isn't reaching customers on the right channels.

4. **Online payment adoption rate** — what percentage of customers are paying via the digital payment link versus check or cash. This is a leading indicator of collection speed. Businesses with **60%+ digital payment adoption** collect 20+ days faster on average than those still primarily collecting by check.

5. **Hours spent on payment tasks per month** — have your admin or office staff track this before and after automation. The target is under 5 hours per month for a business doing 60–100 jobs. If it's still above 15 hours after automation, manual exceptions are piling up and need to be systematized.

Run these numbers every 30 days for the first quarter. Most businesses see meaningful movement on days-to-payment within the first month — faster invoicing alone moves that number before the reminder sequence even kicks in fully.
