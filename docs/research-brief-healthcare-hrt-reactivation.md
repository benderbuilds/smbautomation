# Research Brief: Automating Patient Reactivation and Win-Back for HRT Programs

## Core Pain Point

HRT programs experience a predictable churn pattern: patients cancel or go inactive after 3–6 months, often because they "feel better" and assume they no longer need treatment, experience side effects they didn't communicate, or had a billing issue they never resolved. These are not lost causes — many of these patients will need or want to return to treatment within 6–12 months as symptoms return. But most HRT programs have zero systematic process for re-engaging inactive patients. They're spending 100% of their growth budget on new acquisition and 0% on reactivation.

The business owner's problem: they have a database of hundreds (or thousands) of former patients who already trust the brand, already have medical records on file, and already went through onboarding — but nobody is reaching out to them. Reactivating a former patient costs a fraction of acquiring a new one, yet most programs don't have the staff bandwidth to run manual win-back campaigns.

## Supporting Statistics

- HRT program churn rate averages 35–50% annually, with the highest drop-off at months 3–6 (estimated, based on telehealth subscription benchmarks)
- 60–70% of women who discontinue HRT restart within 2 years as symptoms return (estimated, based on longitudinal HRT adherence studies)
- Reactivating a former patient costs $20–$50 (automated outreach) vs. $150–$400 to acquire a new patient (estimated)
- Win-back campaigns in subscription businesses convert at 10–20% when well-timed and personalized (Recurly State of Subscriptions Report)
- A 500-patient HRT program that reactivates just 15% of its annual churned patients recovers $45,000–$150,000/year in recurring revenue (calculated: 500 × 40% churn × 15% reactivated × $300/month × 5 months average re-tenure)

## Automation Angle

**Patient reactivation automation system:**

1. **Churn classification at exit:** When a patient cancels or goes inactive, the system tags the reason:
   - Feeling better / don't think I need it (highest reactivation potential)
   - Side effects (medium potential — needs clinical follow-up offer)
   - Cost/billing (medium potential — needs pricing/payment flexibility offer)
   - Switched providers (low potential)
   - No reason given (medium potential — treat as "feeling better")

2. **Timed reactivation sequences by reason:**

   **"Feeling better" track (60% of churned patients):**
   - Month 2: Educational email — "What happens when you stop HRT" (symptom return timeline)
   - Month 4: SMS check-in — "How are you feeling? Many patients notice changes around this time"
   - Month 6: Reactivation offer — "Your records are still on file. Restart in 48 hours with no new intake required" + scheduling link
   - Month 9: Final outreach — limited-time pricing or protocol update announcement

   **"Side effects" track:**
   - Month 1: Provider-signed message — "We have new protocol options that may work better for you"
   - Month 3: Educational content about alternative delivery methods (patch vs. oral vs. pellet)
   - Month 5: Reactivation offer with complimentary provider consult

   **"Cost" track:**
   - Week 1: Payment plan options or reduced-tier program offer
   - Month 2: "Prices have been updated" or seasonal promotion
   - Month 4: ROI-focused content — cost of symptoms vs. cost of treatment

3. **Re-onboarding fast track:** Returning patients skip the full intake process — system pulls existing records, checks if labs are current, and gets them to a provider appointment in 24–48 hours instead of 7–14 days.

**Key metrics:**
- Reactivation rate by churn reason
- Time to reactivation (average months between cancel and restart)
- Revenue recovered from reactivated patients per quarter
- Cost per reactivation vs. cost per new acquisition

## Differentiating Angle vs. Generic Health Content

This is NOT about "benefits of continuing HRT" or patient retention tips. This is about **treating your churned patient database as a revenue asset** and building automated systems to harvest it. The reader has hundreds of former patients sitting in a CRM doing nothing, and they haven't done the math on what those patients are worth if even 10% come back.

Frame it as: "Your most profitable 'new' patients are the ones who already cancelled. Here's how to bring them back on autopilot."
